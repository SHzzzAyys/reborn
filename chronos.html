01 · Executive Summary

A document
about noticing.

Chronos is a time-tracking application that treats the act of recording as an act of reflection, not bookkeeping. This document defines the product in full.

The Thesis

The time-tracking category is mature but unloved. Toggl is built for billing. RescueTime is built for surveillance. Daylio is built for emotions but not for time. Day One is built for words but not for data. None of them are built for a person who simply wants to see how their days move.

Chronos sits in this gap. It is small, intentional, and refuses to optimize for engagement. It records time and mood with equal weight. It visualizes a year in a way that fits on one screen. And it ships with privacy as the default, not the upgrade.

The Operating Principles

№ 01

Less is the feature

Every removed surface is a deliberate design choice. Restraint is a competitive advantage in a category drowning in features.

№ 02

The record is the reward

Users will not be reminded, gamified, or guilt-tripped. The visualization itself must be beautiful enough to pull people back.

№ 03

Privacy as architecture

Local-first storage. Encrypted sync only when requested. The server, when it exists, sees ciphertext only.

№ 04

Native to two tongues

Chinese and English are first-class citizens from day one. Not translation—co-design. Content language is independent of UI language.

02 · Product Vision

Time is the only material
we are made of.

Most time-tracking products promise productivity. Chronos promises something stranger and more useful: awareness.

What it does

A user opens Chronos and writes one line: "morning walk." They tap one category—movement. They drag a slider to thirty minutes. They tap one emoji that means today felt good. They put the phone down. The whole interaction lasts under five seconds.

Over weeks, this small habit produces something they did not have before: a picture of their own life that is both honest and beautiful. They see the morning hours they always assumed were lost. They see the week they ran every day, and the next week they did not. They see, with no judgment, where they actually spent their attention.

Who it is for

Chronos is not for everyone, and that is the point. It is built for the 5–10% of people who already keep journals, run, meditate, or otherwise notice themselves. They do not need to be convinced to track. They need a tool that respects what they are doing.

Persona / 用户画像

Liwen, 32, product designer in Shanghai. Keeps a daily Notion log. Reads non-fiction. Wakes at 6:30 to swim. Owns three meditation apps. Frustrated that nothing she's tried for time tracking has felt designed.

Sam, 28, software engineer in Berlin. Bilingual—German at work, English with friends. Cares deeply about data ownership. Has tried Toggl (too businesslike), RescueTime (creepy), and Daylio (too cute). Wants something quiet.

Mei, 41, freelance translator in Taipei. Tracks her own hours but hates spreadsheets. Wants to understand if her freelance income justifies her hours. Already journals in Day One but wants the data layer.

"We don't want to fix your time. We want to help you see it. That's already enough."
Product mantra / 产品咒语

NotReasoning
A productivity toolProductivity is a value judgment. Chronos refuses to tell users whether their day was "good." It only shows them what happened.

04 · Design Philosophy

The rules
we will not break.

Design decisions are not aesthetic preferences. They are operating principles. The following nine rules govern every screen.

  1. № 01

    One screen, one task.

    Home is for recording. Stats is for seeing. Settings is for adjusting. We never blend these.

  2. № 02

    Hide by default.

    Mood, notes, tags, subcategories—none of these appear in the first surface. The user reaches for them.

  3. № 03

    Whitespace earns its keep.

    Empty space is content. We do not fill it because we can.

  4. № 04

    Color signals, never decorates.

    The palette is monochrome with one exception. Color appears when it carries information. Otherwise—gray scale, paper, ink.

  5. № 05

    Animation has a purpose.

    No movement for its own sake. Animation exists to acknowledge a user action or to make a transition legible. 180–240 ms, always eased.

  6. № 06

    Type does the heavy lifting.

    Serif display + sans body. Three weights, no more. The page is structured by typography, not by color or shadow.

  7. № 07

    Tone is dry, not chirpy.

    "Recorded." Not "Yay, you logged it! 🎉". Treat the user as an adult who chose to be here.

  8. № 08

    No streak shaming.

    Missed days are shown as dotted outlines, never as red breaks. We count cumulative days, never consecutive.

  9. № 09

    The data belongs to the user.

    Always exportable. JSON and CSV from day one. No data lock-in, ever.

Field test Before any new feature ships, it must pass: "Does this make the product simpler or more complex in the user's experience?" If the answer is "more complex," it does not ship—even if it is more functional, more competitive, or more requested.

06 · Feature Architecture

Three rooms,
no hallways.

The application has exactly three primary surfaces. No bottom navigation, no hamburger menu—surfaces are reached by direct gesture.

Surface topology

Surface 01
Home

The recording surface

Today's records. A single round button. Nothing else.

  • Date and today's total time, top of screen
  • List of today's records in chronological order
  • Single FAB-style record button, centered bottom
  • Swipe right → Stats, swipe left → Settings
Surface 02
Stats

The reflection surface

Day, week, month, year views. Four toggles, one screen each.

  • Pill toggle: Day · Week · Month · Year
  • Four summary metric cards at the top
  • Primary chart (ring / stacked bar / heatmap / pixel-year)
  • One-line AI observation at the bottom
Surface 03
Settings

The adjustment surface

Categories. Language. Theme. Data. Nothing more.

  • Categories—up to 8, ordered manually
  • Language—Chinese / English / System
  • Appearance—Light / Dark / System
  • Data—Export JSON, Export CSV, Erase all
  • Privacy—Plain-language statement of where data lives

Record entry — the critical flow

  1. 01 Tap + Round button, bottom center of home. 56×56 dp. Single tap. ~0.3 s
  2. 02 Choose mode Two cards: "Start timing" or "Log just now." Tap once. ~0.5 s
  3. 03 Enter name Cursor lands in the input automatically. Type. Autocomplete suggests based on history. ~2 s
  4. 04 Confirm category & duration Pre-filled from history. Tap to accept or change. ~1 s
  5. 05 Optional mood Five circles. Tap one or skip. ~0.5 s
  6. 06 Save Black bar at the bottom. One tap. Animation acknowledges. Returns to home. ~0.5 s

07 · Information Design

Four ways
to see a self.

Day, week, month, year. Each scale reveals a different question. Each chart is engineered to answer one question well.

Day · The donut

Question answered: Where did today's time go, proportionally?

Week · The stacked bar

Question answered: Which days were dense? Which were empty? What does my week shape look like?

Month · The heatmap

Question answered: What is my rhythm across weeks? Where are the dense and sparse stretches?

Year · The pixel grid

Question answered: What did this year look like, all at once?

08 · Signature Visualizations

Three views
that get screenshotted.

Every product needs a moment people share. These are ours. Each one is built to be the picture someone posts when they say "my year, by Chronos."

Signature 01 · The River of Days

Seven rows, one per day of the week. Each row is a horizontal timeline from 0:00 to 24:00. Activities appear as colored segments along the row, with sleep shown in the lightest paper-tone. The result resembles a series of rivers—a week of one person's life seen from above.

Signature 02 · The Mirror

A vertical centerline splits the screen. To the left, last month's category bars extend leftward. To the right, this month's bars extend rightward. A directional arrow per category indicates growth or contraction.

Signature 03 · The Pixel Year

Twelve months, each rendered as a small grid of mood-shaded pixels. The year, in one image, on one screen. Below, four small cards: average mood, brightest month, longest streak, deepest dip.

10 · Automation Strategy

Make recording
nearly invisible.

A five-level automation ladder reduces the cost of recording without ever removing the user's awareness of what they are recording.

LevelMechanismFriction reduced toPrivacy costShips in
L1Smart autocomplete~3 sNone (local fuzzy match)v1.0
L2Context triggers (geofences, evening prompt)~1 sLow (all local)v1.1
L3System data import (calendar, health)Confirmation onlyLow (read-only, local)v1.2
L4Retrospective sliders (nightly mode)~30 s for the whole dayNonev1.3
L5AI inference (voice, freeform text)~5 s to talkMedium (on-device model preferred)v2.0
Principle 100% automation is the wrong target. The right target is making the user's decision-making feel effortless while preserving their decision-making. We never log without confirmation.

12 · Privacy Framework

A wall
between us and the data.

Privacy is not a feature in Chronos. It is the architecture. The product is engineered so that, even if we wanted to read user data, we technically cannot.

TierWhere data livesWho can read itDefault for
L1 — Local onlyEncrypted SQLite on user's deviceOnly the user (via the app)Every new user, forever, by default
L2 — Encrypted syncUser's device + opaque blobs on our serverOnly the user (we hold ciphertext)Users who opt in to multi-device
L3 — Server-readablen/an/aNever. We do not offer this tier.
№ 01

No account at first launch

The user can use Chronos forever without registering. No email, password, or phone field anywhere unless they request sync.

№ 02

A visible privacy panel

Settings → Privacy displays a plain-language summary: where your data is, what's encrypted, and your key's fingerprint.

№ 03

Always exportable

JSON and CSV export buttons in the data section. No "premium tier" gates export.

№ 04

Account deletion is instant

One button. Confirmation dialog. Server-side wipe within minutes.

13 · Development Roadmap

Thirty-two weeks
to a shipped product.

A part-time pace: ten to twelve hours per week, eight months end-to-end, with one rest week every four.

Months 1–2
Foundation

Polish v0.1 → v0.3

Take the existing web prototype to a mobile-first design with the gray palette properly applied.

  • Mobile-first single-column layout
  • Six-step gray ramp on category chips
  • All four stats views functional
  • JSON and CSV export
Months 3–4
Native

Port to native — Flutter mobile build

Re-implement the web design as a Flutter app for iOS, with feature parity.

  • Flutter project scaffolding with i18n, theming
  • SQLite + SQLCipher integration
  • Native iOS animations and transitions
  • Self-use for two weeks straight without breaking
Months 5–6
Differentiators

The differentiators

Polish, signature, and bilingual completion.

  • River of Days chart — fully implemented
  • Blue accent year heatmap with tooltips
  • Smart autocomplete (L1 automation)
  • Full Chinese / English parity
  • Dark mode, all transitions tuned to 180–240ms
Month 7
Beta

Beta — TestFlight closed

Twenty to fifty hand-picked users. Daily feedback ingestion. No new features.

  • App Store screenshots, copy, metadata
  • TestFlight submission and distribution
  • Track: D7 retention, daily records per user
Month 8
Launch

Public launch — App Store, v1.0

Hold the release for a Tuesday. Coordinate with build-in-public posts. Then take a week off.

  • App Store live in CN, US, UK, JP, DE, AU regions
  • Launch posts on X, Indie Hackers, 小红书, Hacker News
  • Mandatory rest week post-launch

The note to return to / 给低谷的自己
№ 01

An accountability partner

One person, same boat. A Sunday-night message exchange. Not advice—witness.

№ 02

A weekly video diary

30 seconds, every Saturday. Watch month one's video in month four. Progress is undeniable.

№ 03

A founding document

Why you started, written before the dip. Three to five honest reasons. Open it during month five.

№ 04

An AI companion

Continue using Claude or similar for product reasoning, code review, and emotional throughput. Available always, infinitely patient.
